# Detecting Revenue Leakage
A data analytics case study on transaction failure, data integrity, and revenue risk across a multi-country digital payments platform.

## Executive Summary
This project analyzes 1,005,000 digital transactions worth a combined $4.78B, generated by 89,998 customers across five markets (Nigeria, Kenya, Ghana, UK, USA) between January 2024 and December 2025. The analysis was framed as real-world fintech risk audit: before any business insight can be trusted, the data itself has to be interrogated. That audit surfaced 18% of all transactions failing to complete successfully representing roughly $860M in failed or pending transaction value alongside structural data quality gaps (missing fields, corrupted dates, duplicate records) that would silently distort reporting if left unresolved. The end deliverable is a cleaned, trustworthy dataset, a documented cleaning methodology, and a set of prioritized, and revenue-linked recommendations.

## Business Problem
"Why is a meaningful share of our transaction volume failing to convert into completed revenue, and can we trust our data enough to act on it?"
 The leadership team of Palmpay suspects that the platform is losing revenue to failed and pending transactions and also lacks confidence in the underlying data to quantify it. Stakeholders need answers to:
1.	How much revenue is at risk from failed, pending, and refunded transactions?
2.	Which country, payment method, or channel is risk concentrated?
3.	Is the data reliable enough to build an executive dashboard on, or does it need remediation first?
4.	What should the business do next to reduce failure rates and protect revenue?

## Methodology
A five-stage, reproducible pipeline is used, the same structure applied to production ETL/data quality work:
Stage	Actions
  1. Profile	Audited all 10 columns for nulls, duplicates, type mismatches, and outliers across 1M+ rows
  2. Diagnose	Classified each issue as structural (e.g., merchant is legitimately blank for a Deposit) vs. true data defect (e.g corrupted date strings,   duplicate transaction IDs)
  3. Clean	Standardized date formats, removed exact duplicates, flagged negative/anomalous amounts for review rather than silently dropping them
  4. Analyze	Segmented transaction outcomes by country, payment method, device, and time to isolate where failure risk concentrates
  5. Communicate	Translated findings into a dashboard-ready summary and prioritized business recommendations

Key Finding: The Failure Problem Is Systemic, Not Regional
Failure and pending rates land within 0.2 percentage points of each other across every country (12.9–13.1% combined) and all four payment methods (13%) indicating the issue is not localized to one region or rail, but is likely a systemic platform or processing issue (e.g., timeout handling, retry logic, or downstream banking-partner reliability) rather than a market-specific problem. 

## Correction to an Earlier Assumption
An earlier draft insight claimed "USSD dominance in mobile-money markets vs. Card usage elsewhere." I checked against the data, this doesn't hold. Payment method share is 25% Card / 25% Transfer / 25% USSD / 25% Wallet in every single country, including Nigeria and Kenya. That pattern is consistent with a synthetically generated, uniformly randomized dataset rather than real regional payment behavior, and the dashboard reports the verified split instead.
## Recommendations to the business:
1.	Investigate /market-specific fixes.
2.	Recover the $619M at-risk pipeline — Build automated retry logic for "Pending" transactions before they lapse into "Failed," which alone could recover a meaningful share of the $237M currently stuck in Pending.
3.	Fix data capture at the source — The payment method and device fields are missing on 20–25% of records; this isn't just a reporting inconvenience, it's blocking root-cause analysis of why transactions fail.
4.	Establish a recurring data quality SLA — Bake duplicate/invalid-date checks into the ingestion pipeline so this audit doesn't need to be repeated manually every quarter.
5.	Institute the Data Trust Score as a standing dashboard tile so any future metric is reported alongside its confidence level.
